1. Set theory and basic probability rules

Problem 1

Suppose the events \(A\) and \(B\) are disjoint. Under what conditions are \(A^{c}\) and \(B^{c}\) also disjoint events?

Problem 2

Let \(A\), \(B\), and \(C\) be three arbitrary events, and let \(D\) denote the event that “exactly one of these three events occurs.”

  1. Draw a cartoon of \(A\), \(B\), \(C\), and \(D\);
  2. Use the three basic set operations (union, intersection, and complement) to express \(D\) in terms of \(A\), \(B\), and \(C\);
  3. Show that

\[P(D)=P(A) + P(B) + P(C) - 2P(A \cap B) - 2P(A \cap C) - 2P(B \cap C) + 3P(A \cap B \cap C).\]

2. Counting

Problem 3

The United States Senate contains two senators – one senior and one junior – from each of the \(50\) states.

  1. If a committee of eight senators is selected, what is the probability that it will contain at least one of the two senators from a certain specified state?
  2. If a committee of twenty senators is selected, what is the probability it will contain \(15\) junior senators and \(5\) senior senators?
  3. What is the probability that a group of \(50\) senators selected at random will contain one senator from each state?

Problem 4

Congratulations! You and two of your friends are now proud owners of brand-new library cards. In order to use your new cards, all you and your friends need to do is create four-digit PINs, with numbers \(0\) - \(9\) available (in theory) at each position. Predictably, all of you have different ideas of what an acceptable PIN is (odd or even, leading zeros or not, etc.).

  1. You are fairly chill about what you want in a PIN – all you want is an even PIN, and you’re totally fine if it starts with a zero. How many possible PINs fit your preference?
  2. One of your friends is a bit more persnickety – they want a PIN divisible by \(5\), and they cannot stand PINs starting with a zero. How many possible PINs fit this friend’s preferences?
  3. Your other friend has some more…particular preferences – they have an affinity for the number \(16\) and thus want the digits \(1\) and \(6\) to appear in that exact order at some point in the PIN. Further, they also despise PINs starting with a zero. How many possible PINs fit this friend’s preferences?

3. Conditional probability and independence

Problem 5

An urn contains 5 white and 10 black balls. Four tickets labeled 1, 2, 3, and 4 reside in a box. A ticket is drawn at random from the box, and that number of balls is randomly chosen from the urn, without replacement.

  1. What is the probability that all of the balls drawn are white?
  2. If we learn that all balls drawn from the urn are white, what is the conditional probability that the #3 ticket was drawn?

Problem 6

  1. Let \(S\) be the set of all permutations of \(a\), \(b\), \(c\), together with the triples \(aaa\), \(bbb\), and \(ccc\). Imagine we draw an outcome randomly from this set. Define the event \(A_k=\{\text{the kth spot is occupied by the letter a}\}\) for \(k=1,\,2,\,3\). Compute the probabilities \(P(A_k)\), \(P(A_i\cap A_j)\), \(P(A_1\cap A_2\cap A_3)\), and comment on the independence of the three of events;

  2. Imagine we roll two fair, six-sided die. Consider the events \[ \begin{aligned} A&=\text{first die is 1, 2, 3}\\ B&=\text{first die is 3, 4, 5}\\ C&=\text{the sum of the two rolls is 9}. \end{aligned} \] Compute \(P(A)\), \(P(B)\), \(P(C)\), \(P(A\cap B)\), \(P(A\cap C)\), \(P(B\cap C)\), \(P(A\cap B\cap C)\), and comment on the independence of the three events.

4. Discrete random variables

Problem 7

Let \(A_1\), \(A_2\), \(A_3\), \(A_4\), \(A_5\), and \(A_6\) be events in some sample space, each with the same probability \(0<p<1/6\). Define the indicator random variables

\[ I_k = \begin{cases} 0 & \text{if }A_k^c\text{ happens}\\ 1 & \text{if }A_k\text{ happens}, \end{cases} \]

and let \(X\) be their sum

\[ X = I_1 + I_2 + I_3 + I_4+I_5+I_6. \]

  1. Assume the events \(A_i\) are mutually disjoint and compute…

    1. \(E(X)\).
    2. \(P(X=1)\).
    3. \(P(X=3)\).

  2. Assume the \(A_i\) are independent and compute…

    1. \(E(X)\).
    2. \(P(X=1)\).
    3. \(P(X=3)\).

Problem 8

Signore Fibonacci has created a a balanced, six-sided die whose faces are labeled, naturally, 1, 2, 3, 5, 8, 13. He gives a pair to Enzo, who rolls them repeatedly, eager to get double 13s. Let \(X\) be the number of times he has to roll the pair of dice in order to see double 13s for the first time.

  1. What is the pmf of \(X\)?
  2. What is the expected value of \(X\)?
  3. Find a closed-form expression for \(P(X\geq m)\).

5. Absolutely continuous random variables

Problem 9

Here is a cdf for some nonnegative random variable \(X\):

\[ F(x) = \begin{cases} 1-\exp(-\sqrt[3]{x}) & x\geq 0 \\ 0 & \text{else}. \end{cases} \]

  1. What is \(P(1 < X <8)\)?
  2. What is the pdf of \(X\)?
  3. Compute \(E(X^n)\) for any \(n\in\mathbb{N}\).
  4. What is the variance of \(X\)?

Problem 10

Consider this function:

\[ F(x) = \begin{cases} 1 - e^{-g(x)} & x\geq 0\\ 0 & \text{else}. \end{cases} \]

Assume \(g(x)\) is a continuous function. What properties must \(g(x)\) satisfy in order for \(F(x)\) to be a valid cumulative distribution function (CDF)? Give three examples of functions \(g(x)\) that satisfy these properties.

Problem 11

Here is the goofy pdf of some nonnegative random variable \(X\):

\[ f(x) = e^{1-e^x+x},\quad x\geq 0. \]

  1. Compute the cdf of \(X\);
  2. Compute the median of \(X\).

6. The moment-generating function

Problem 12

A certain random variable \(X\) has moment-generating function

\[ M(t)=\frac{e^{2t}}{1-3t},\quad t<1/3. \]

  1. Compute the mean and variance of \(X\).
  2. What is the moment generating function of the new random variable

\[ Y=\frac{\pi}{2}-\frac{X}{3}. \]

Problem 13

The binomial theorem says that

\[ (x+y)^n=\sum\limits_{k=0}^n\binom{n}{k}x^{n-k}y^k,\quad x,\,y\in\mathbb{R}\text{ and }n\in\mathbb{N}. \]

If, instead, we consider negative powers, you get this funky thing:

\[ (x+y)^{-n}=\sum\limits_{k=0}^\infty(-1)^k\binom{n+k-1}{k}y^kx^{-n-k},\quad |y|<x\text{ and }n\in\mathbb{N}. \]

Now, let \(X\) be a discrete random variable with \(\text{Range}(X)=\mathbb{N}=\{0,\,1,\,2,\,...\}\) and pmf

\[ P(X = k) = \binom{k+r-1}{k}(1-p)^kp^r,\quad k\in\mathbb{N}. \]

This is the negative binomial distribution, denoted \(X\sim\text{NB}(r,\,p)\), where \(0<p<1\) and \(r=1,\,2,\,3,\,...\) are parameters.

  1. What is \((1-p)^{-r}\)?
  2. What is the mgf of \(X\)?
  3. Use the mgf to compute the mean of \(X\).
  4. Let \(X_1\), \(X_2\), …, \(X_n\) be independent random variables each with distribution \(\text{NB}(r_i,\,p)\). What is the distribution of \(S_n=\sum_{i=1}^nX_i\)?

7. Transformations

Problem 14

Let \(X\sim\text{Gamma}(\alpha,\,\beta)\), and define a new random variable \(Y=1/X\)?

  1. What is the density of \(Y\)?
  2. What is \(E(Y)\)?
  3. What is \(\text{var}(Y)\)?
Note

A random variable possessing this new density is said to follow the inverse gamma distribution.

Problem 15

Let \(X\) be a nonnegative continuous random variable with this density:

\[ f(x\,|\,\theta)=\theta(x+1)^{-(\theta+1)},\quad x>0. \]

Treat \(\theta>0\) as a constant, and define a new random variable \(Y=\ln(X+1)\).

  1. What is the range of \(Y\)?
  2. What is the density of \(Y\)?
  3. Is the density of \(Y\) familiar?

Problem 16

Let \(X\) be a continuous random variable with this density:

\[ f(x\,|\,\theta) = \frac{1}{2\theta}\exp\left(-\frac{|x|}{\theta}\right), \quad -\infty<x<\infty. \]

Treat \(\theta>0\) as a constant, and define a new random variable \(Y=|X|\).

  1. Sketch what this density looks like for \(\theta=1\).
  2. What is the range of \(Y\)?
  3. What is the density of \(Y\)?
  4. Is the density of \(Y\) familiar?

8. Joint distributions

Problem 17

Here is the joint density for a random pair:

\[ f_{XY}(x,\,y) = \frac{24}{7} x(2-y),\quad 0<x<1\text{ and }0<y<1-x. \]

  1. Sketch the joint range of \((X,\,Y)\).
  2. Find the marginal density of \(Y\).
  3. Find the marginal density of \(X\).
  4. Find the conditional density of \(X\) given \(Y=y\).
  5. Use this to compute \(P(X>1/2\,|\,Y=1/3)\).
  6. What is \(E(X\,|\,Y=y\)?

Problem 18

Consider the joint distribution of random variables \(X\) and \(Y\), written in hierarchical form:

\[ \begin{aligned} X&\sim\textrm{Gamma}\left(\frac{d_2}{2},\, \frac{d_2}{2}\right)\\ Y\,|\, X = x&\sim\textrm{Gamma}\left(\frac{d_1}{2},\, \frac{d_1}{2}x\right).\\ \end{aligned} \]

Do some serious “massage and squint” to show that the marginal pdf of \(Y\) is

\[ f_Y(y)=\frac{\Gamma\left(\frac{d_1}{2}+\frac{d_2}{2}\right)}{\Gamma\left(\frac{d_1}{2}\right)\Gamma\left(\frac{d_2}{2}\right)}\left(\frac{d_1}{d_2}\right)^{\frac{d_1}{2}}y^{\frac{d_1}{2}-1}\left(1+\frac{d_1}{d_2}y\right)^{-\frac{d_1+d_2}{2}},\quad y>0. \]

This means that \(Y\) has the F-distribution.

9. Maximum likelihood estimation

Problem 19

Let \(X_1\), \(X_2\), …, \(X_n\) be iid from some member of the parametric family that you studied in Problem 15 above:

\[ f(x\,|\,\theta)=\theta(x+1)^{-(\theta+1)},\quad x>0. \]

  1. What is the maximum likelihood estimator of \(\theta>0\)?
  2. What is the sampling distribution of the estimator? Problem 14 might be helpful here.
  3. What is the MSE of the estimator?
  4. State the bounds of a 90% confidence interval for \(\theta\).

Problem 20

Let \(X_1\), \(X_2\), …, \(X_n\) be iid from some member of the parametric family that you studied in Problem 16 above:

\[ f(x\,|\,\theta) = \frac{1}{2\theta}\exp\left(-\frac{|x|}{\theta}\right), \quad -\infty<x<\infty. \]

  1. What is the maximum likelihood estimator of \(\theta>0\)?
  2. What is the sampling distribution of the estimator?
  3. What is the MSE of the estimator?
  4. State the bounds of a 75% confidence interval for \(\theta\).

10. Bayesian inference

Problem 21

Consider the following Bayesian model:

\[ \begin{aligned} p&\sim\text{Beta}(a_0,\,b_0)&& \text{(prior)}\\ X_1,\,X_2,\,...,\,X_n\,|\,p&\overset{\text{iid}}{\sim}\text{Geometric}(p).&& \text{(likelihood)} \end{aligned} \]

  1. What is the posterior distribution?
  2. Compute the posterior mean and show that it is a weighted average of the prior mean and the maximum likelihood estimator.
  3. State an 82% credible interval for \(p\).

Problem 22

Recall this family of distributions from Problems 15 and 19 above:

\[ f(x\,|\,\theta)=\theta(x+1)^{-(\theta+1)},\quad x>0. \]

If we observed iid data from some unknown member of this family, we could perform Bayesian inference for the unknown parameter \(\theta>0\) with the following model:

\[ \begin{aligned} \theta&\sim\text{Gamma}(a_0,\,b_0) && \text{(prior)}\\ X_1,\,X_2,\,...,\,X_n\,|\,\theta&\overset{\text{iid}}{\sim}f(x\,|\,\theta). && \text{(likelihood)} \end{aligned} \]

  1. What is the posterior distribution of \(\theta\)?
  2. Compute the posterior mean and show that it is a convex combination of the prior mean and the mle.
  3. State a 90% credible interval for \(\theta\).
